# How to Prompt Effectively (Simple, Not Technical)

## What you'll learn

- Why most prompts produce bad results — and the simple fix
- A beginner-friendly prompt formula you can use immediately
- How to follow up when AI gives you something that misses the mark

## Why it matters

Prompting is a skill. But it is not a complicated skill. You do not need to study "prompt engineering." You need to understand a few basic principles, and everything you get from AI will improve immediately.

## Explanation

A bad prompt is vague. A good prompt is specific.

That is almost the entire lesson. But let us break down exactly what "specific" means.

A strong prompt answers four questions:

**1. What do you want AI to create?**
Be clear about the format. "Give me a list" is different from "write a guide" which is different from "create an outline."

**2. Who is it for?**
Describe the audience. "For complete beginners" gives AI a very different target than "for experienced marketers."

**3. What is the goal or outcome?**
What should the reader be able to do after reading it? "So they can set up their first product in one hour" tells AI what success looks like.

**4. Any specific constraints?**
Length, tone, format, things to include or avoid. "Keep it under 300 words" or "use simple language, no jargon" shapes the output significantly.

The formula is: [What to create] + [Who it is for] + [What it should achieve] + [Any constraints]

## Step-by-step

1. Before typing anything, answer the four questions above in your head
2. Write your prompt using the formula
3. Read the output — is it close to what you wanted?
4. If not, add a follow-up message that says "That was close, but please [specific change]"
5. Keep refining in the same conversation until you get what you need

## Example

**Weak prompt:**
"Write something about using AI for freelancers."

**Strong prompt:**
"Write a short guide (under 400 words) for freelancers who have never used AI before. The goal is to help them understand three simple ways AI can save them time this week. Use plain language and avoid technical terms."

The second prompt will produce something you can actually use. The first prompt will produce something generic that does not serve anyone.

**Follow-up example:**
If AI gives you something too formal, you can follow up with:
"That is good but the tone is too stiff. Rewrite it in a conversational tone, like you are talking to a friend."

## Action task

Take your product idea from Module 2 and write a strong prompt using the four-part formula.

Write it out fully before you open your AI tool. Then paste it in and see what you get.

If the result is not quite right, practise the follow-up: "That is close, but please make it [more specific / shorter / simpler / etc.]"

Do this at least twice in one session. You will feel the difference immediately.

Save the best prompt you write. You will use it in your actual build session in Module 4. The next lesson sets up the toolkit you need so that when you sit down to build, everything is ready and nothing slows you down.

## Common mistake

Accepting the first output without any follow-up. AI almost never produces exactly what you need on the first attempt. The magic is in the follow-up prompts. Think of it as a conversation, not a single request.
