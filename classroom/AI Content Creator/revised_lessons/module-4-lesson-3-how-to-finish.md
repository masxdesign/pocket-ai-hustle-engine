# How to Finish (Anti-Procrastination)

## What you'll learn

- Why most digital assets never get finished and how to avoid that trap
- Three practical techniques for pushing through to completion
- How to decide when your product is "done enough" to publish

## Why it matters

Starting is the hardest part for most people. But finishing is where a lot of progress dies too. You are 70% done and suddenly everything else feels more urgent. This lesson keeps you moving to the finish line.

## Explanation

Procrastination on creative projects usually comes from one of three places:

**1. The project feels too big.**
Solution: Break it into the smallest possible next step. Not "finish the guide." Just "write the intro paragraph." Once you start, momentum carries you forward.

**2. The internal critic shows up.**
"This is not good enough. Nobody will want this. I should start over." This voice is loudest when you are close to finishing. It is not telling the truth. Keep going.

**3. Perfectionism disguised as quality control.**
Tweaking the same section for the fourth time is not improving quality. It is avoiding completion. Set a rule: once a section has been edited twice, move on.

The goal is not a perfect product. The goal is a finished product that you can share, get feedback on, and improve based on real information.

## Step-by-step

1. Break your remaining work into tasks of 15 minutes or less
2. Do one task. Just one. Do not think about the whole product.
3. Set a "done enough" definition before you start your next session: "This product is done when it has [X sections] and has been read through once"
4. When you hit your definition, stop editing and move to publishing
5. You can always release version 2

## Example

A student was building a checklist and kept rewriting the same five items for three days. She was not making it better — she was avoiding the finish line.

She used the "two-edit rule." Once she had edited each item twice, she moved on. She finished the checklist in one more 45-minute session.

She published it. The feedback she got told her which two items were confusing. She updated it a week later. Version 2 was better than anything she would have created by continuing to rewrite in isolation.

Real feedback from real readers beats theoretical perfection every time.

## Action task

Look at where you are with your current product. Identify the single next task — not the next three tasks, just one.

Write it down: "The next thing I will do is [specific small task]."

Now set a timer for 25 minutes and do only that task.

After 25 minutes, write the next task. Repeat until the product is done.

When it is done — when it solves the problem it promises to solve, reads clearly, and is complete — move straight to Module 5. That is where you publish it and put it in front of real people.

## Common mistake

Setting "perfect" as your definition of done. Done means: it solves the problem it promises to solve, it is readable, and it is complete. That is it. Design, length, polish — these can all be improved later. Ship it.
