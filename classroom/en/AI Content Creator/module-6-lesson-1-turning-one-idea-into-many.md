# Turning One Idea Into Multiple Assets

## What you'll learn

- How one product idea can become five or more separate assets
- The simple expansion framework for multiplying your output
- How to build a small product ecosystem without starting from scratch each time

## Why it matters

You have built something. You have published it. You have shared it and collected feedback. That first product is not just a product — it is a foundation. Everything you learned building it, every piece of feedback you received, every question someone asked — that is your raw material for the next five products. This lesson shows you exactly how to use what you already have to build what comes next, without starting from scratch.

## Explanation

One idea has multiple formats. One format serves one type of person in one situation. A different format serves a different person in a different situation.

Here is the expansion framework:

**Start with your core idea.**
Example: "Helping beginners use ChatGPT more effectively."

**Then expand in four directions:**

1. **Go deeper** — take one section of your existing product and turn it into its own product. A checklist becomes a guide. A guide chapter becomes a mini ebook.

2. **Go broader** — take your topic and apply it to a different audience. "ChatGPT for freelancers" becomes "ChatGPT for small business owners" or "ChatGPT for job seekers."

3. **Change the format** — the same content in a different format serves a different need. A guide becomes a checklist. A checklist becomes a prompt pack. A prompt pack becomes a template.

4. **Go further along the journey** — your first product covers step one. Your second product covers step two. Your third product covers what to do after step two.

You are not copying yourself. You are serving your reader at different stages of their journey.

## Step-by-step

1. Look at your finished product
2. List three sections or topics that could each become their own smaller product
3. Ask: "What does my reader need to know after they have used this product?"
4. Ask: "Is there a different audience who has the same problem?"
5. Write down two or three ideas that came from this exercise — these are your next products

## Example

A student created a prompt pack for freelance writers.

From that one product, she identified:

- A checklist: "10 things to do before sending a client proposal" (same audience, different format)
- A guide: "How to use AI to write your freelance portfolio in a weekend" (same audience, next step in their journey)
- A prompt pack for copywriters: same format, adjacent audience
- A template: a client email template pack built from the prompts in her original product

Four new products. All built from the foundation of her first one.

She did not have four new ideas. She had one idea, expanded in four directions.

## Action task

Take the product you just built and published. Apply the four expansion directions to it specifically — not a hypothetical product, your actual one.

Write one potential product for each:

- Go deeper: what section of your product could become its own standalone product?
- Go broader: what other audience has the same problem you just solved?
- Change the format: what would your product look like in a different format?
- Go further along the journey: what does your reader need to know next?

You now have four product ideas, all rooted in something real you already built. Pick the one that excites you most. That is your next build. The following lessons will help you build it consistently, without burning out, and turn this into a system that runs.

## Common mistake

Building four new products at once. Expand one at a time. Finish one, publish it, get feedback, then start the next. Trying to build everything simultaneously means finishing nothing.
