# Avoiding Burnout

## What you'll learn

- The real causes of burnout when building digital products
- How to pace yourself for long-term output
- Simple habits that keep you energised and productive over months, not just days

## Why it matters

Many beginners start with a burst of energy and then crash. They over-commit, do not see instant results, and stop entirely. Avoiding burnout is not about working less — it is about working in a way that is sustainable so you can keep going long enough for the results to appear.

## Explanation

Burnout when building digital products usually comes from one of four things:

**1. Unrealistic expectations**
Expecting to make significant income from your first product in the first week. When it does not happen, the motivation collapses. Adjust the timeline: real momentum builds over months, not days.

**2. Comparison**
Watching creators who have been building for years and measuring your week-one results against their year-five results. This is a guaranteed way to feel inadequate. Focus on your own progress only.

**3. Building without rest**
Treating every available hour as a build session and leaving no space for rest, fun, or other interests. Creative output requires rest. You cannot produce well if you never stop.

**4. Building things you do not care about**
Chasing trends or building products for audiences you have no connection to. If you are not genuinely interested in the topic, the work will feel hollow and you will stop.

The antidote to burnout is a sustainable pace, realistic expectations, and building things that actually interest you.

## Step-by-step

1. Set a realistic monthly output goal: one to two products per month is plenty for a beginner
2. Build a weekly rhythm that includes both work sessions and clear rest days
3. Unfollow or mute accounts that make you feel behind or inadequate
4. Each month, review what you have built — not what you have not yet built
5. Build products that connect to something you genuinely find interesting

## Example

A freelancer went hard for three weeks — building every evening and weekend. She published two products. Then she crashed and did nothing for six weeks.

She came back with a different approach: one 90-minute session twice a week, every week, no exceptions. No sprints, no crashes.

Six months later, she had eight products and consistent weekly output. The tortoise approach worked. The sprint approach did not.

Slow and steady is not a consolation prize. It is the actual strategy.

## Action task

Write down your sustainable weekly commitment. Be honest about what you can actually maintain for six months, not just what you can do this week when you are motivated.

Example: "I will build for 90 minutes on Tuesday evenings and Saturday mornings."

Write it down. Put it in your calendar. Protect it, but do not exceed it in the first month.

## Common mistake

Setting an ambitious schedule based on your most motivated day. The schedule needs to work on your worst day, not your best. A schedule you can keep when you are tired and distracted is worth ten schedules you abandon after two weeks.
