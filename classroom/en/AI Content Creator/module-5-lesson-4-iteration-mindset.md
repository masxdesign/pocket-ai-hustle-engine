# The Iteration Mindset

## What you'll learn

- Why version 1 is a starting point, not a final product
- How to think in iterations rather than single launches
- How the iteration mindset removes pressure and builds momentum

## Why it matters

One of the most useful shifts you can make as a beginner creator is moving from "launch and hope" to "launch and learn." When you see every product as version 1, publishing stops being scary and starts being exciting.

## Explanation

Every successful product you admire has been through dozens of versions. What you see now is the result of iteration — of releasing, getting feedback, improving, and releasing again.

Your first version does not have to succeed on its own merits. It has to get you the information you need to build version 2.

This completely changes how you approach publishing.

Instead of:
"I need this to be perfect before anyone sees it"

You think:
"I need to get this out so I can find out what to improve"

The iteration mindset in practice:

- Version 1: Build the core idea. Get it out. See what happens.
- Version 2: Fix the two biggest gaps. Improve the weakest sections. Re-share.
- Version 3: Add what people keep asking for. Improve based on what is actually being used.
- Version 4+: At this point, you have a genuinely strong product built on real user insight.

Most people never get to version 2 because they are still trying to perfect version 1 before anyone sees it.

## Step-by-step

1. After publishing, wait one week and collect any feedback
2. Write a simple version log: "V1 — initial release. Known gaps: [list them]"
3. After 2–4 weeks, update the product with your top three improvements
4. Re-share the updated version with a note: "Updated with [new thing]"
5. Repeat

## Example

A creator released a beginner AI workflow guide. Version 1 was five pages, quite thin.

Version 2, three weeks later, added a section on prompting that people kept asking about.
Version 3, a month after that, added examples for three different types of users.
Version 4, two months in, added a one-page quick reference card.

By version 4, the guide was strong enough to sell consistently at $19. Version 1 sold for free and helped him understand what to build.

That is the compounding power of iteration. You cannot plan your way to version 4. You have to ship version 1 first.

## Action task

Before you publish your product, write this at the top of a private note:

"V1 — [date]. This is a starting point. My plan is to release version 2 within 30 days based on feedback."

Setting this expectation in advance removes the pressure of perfection. You have already decided this is not the final version.

## Common mistake

Treating each product as a one-time effort. A product you publish once and never update is a dead end. Products that are regularly improved stay relevant, attract more downloads, and build trust over time. Schedule a review of your product every 30 days.
